How to keep Carnatic tala

Carnatic tala is kept with the hands using three building blocks called angas. Each akshara (beat) is shown as a box above; follow the highlighted box and make the matching gesture.

Clap (beat 1 / sam) Finger count / clap Wave (palm up — visarjita)

The three angas (gesture units)

AngaSymbolCountsHow to show it
LaghuI3 / 4 / 5 / 7 / 9 (the jati)One clap, then count remaining beats on the fingers (little finger first)
DrutamO2A clap, then a wave (back of the palm down)
AnudrutamU1A single clap
Jati changes only the length of the laghu. Gati (nadai) is how many equal pulses you subdivide each beat into — switch on “Click subdivisions” to hear them.

Adi tala = Chatusra-jati Triputa (8 aksharas: clap + 3 fingers, clap + wave, clap + wave) — the most common tala in Carnatic music.

For the novice student

Start with Adi tala at a slow tempo (around 50–60 BPM). Watch the boxes and physically clap, count and wave along — do not just listen. Say the count aloud (“1, 2, 3, 4 — clap, wave — clap, wave”). Once steady, switch on subdivisions (try Chatusra gati) to feel the inner pulse, then slowly raise the tempo using the Tempo trainer (e.g. +5 BPM every 4 cycles).

Next learn Rupaka and the two chapu talas (Misra Chapu = 7, Khanda Chapu = 5), which appear constantly in concerts.

For the accompanying expert

Use it as a steady nadai reference for practising kanakku (calculations), korvais and eduppu (off-beat starts). Set the gati to rehearse tisra/khanda/misra nadai passages against a fixed kala-pramanam. Turn off the visual highlight reliance and use only the sam accent so your internal clock leads. The tempo trainer is useful for building speed on brisk tani-avartanam phrases.

How to keep Hindustani taal

A Hindustani taal is a fixed cycle (avartan) of matras (beats) grouped into vibhags (sections). You keep it with claps (tali) and a wave (khali), reciting the drum syllables (bols) of the theka.

Sam (X) — first beat, the resolution point Tali — a numbered clap Khali (0) — wave of the hand

The markers

MarkerSignWhat you do
SamXThe strongest beat — clap. Phrases resolve here; the tabla/dancer “lands” on sam.
Tali2, 3…A clap that marks the start of a filled vibhag.
Khali0An “empty” section, shown by a silent wave of the palm instead of a clap — felt as a relaxation.

Common taals

TaalMatrasVibhagsUsed for

Dadra63+3Light classical, thumri, bhajan
Chautaal126×2Dhrupad / pakhawaj
Dhamar145+2+3+4Dhamar / dhrupad
Rupak is unusual: its sam is also a khali — the cycle begins on the wave, not a clap.
For the novice student

Begin with Teentaal at a medium tempo. Recite the theka aloud while clapping on tali and waving on khali (beat 9): “Dha Dhin Dhin Dha (clap) … Dha Tin Tin Ta (wave) …”. The point is to always feel where sam is. Once comfortable, try Keherwa and Dadra for lighter genres, then Jhaptaal and Rupak for the uneven cycles.

For the accompanying expert

Use it as a lehra/nagma-style steady reference for riyaz, solo tabla, or kathak practice. For vilambit work, lower the BPM and switch on subdivisions to keep the long matras even. The bol display follows the theka so you can lock the laggi/relevant fillers to sam. Set tisra subdivision to drill tihais and layakari (double/triple time) against a fixed lay.

Shruti for the absolute beginner

Shruti means your pitch — your Sa, the tonic that everything else is measured against. A shruti box (or tanpura) holds that Sa steadily so your ear has a fixed anchor. “Keeping shruti” means staying in tune relative to that Sa; it is the single most important habit in Indian music.

1 · Choose your Sa

Sa is not a fixed frequency — each singer picks their own. Hum your most comfortable, relaxed low note and match the drone’s Sa to it. As a rough guide: many men sit around C–D, many women and children around G–A. Pick one Sa and stick with it for weeks so your voice builds muscle memory. Instrumentalists tune their Sa to the instrument.

2 · Pa or Ma?

The drone always sounds Sa (and usually the octave Sa). The other voice is normally Pa (the fifth) — leave “Pa” on for the vast majority of ragas. Switch to Ma (the fourth) only for ragas that omit Pa (e.g. Malkauns / those that lean on Ma). Beginners: start with Sa + Pa.

3 · Lock your voice to the drone

  1. Start the drone and just listen for a few seconds — let the Sa fill your ear.
  2. Hum a soft “Saaa” and slide your pitch until the wobble/roughness between your voice and the drone disappears. When you are exactly in tune the sound becomes smooth and “locks”. That beating you hear when slightly off is your tuning feedback — chase the smoothness.
  3. Hold it. Then sing up the scale Sa Ri Ga Ma Pa Dha Ni Sa and back, checking each note against the ever-present Sa. Sa and Pa should feel rock-solid; use them as checkpoints.

4 · How to maintain shruti (stay in tune)

  • Always practise with the drone on. Never sing or play long passages without a reference Sa.
  • Do daily long tones (akaar): hold Sa, then Pa, then Sa for as long as your breath lasts, keeping zero beating against the drone. 5–10 minutes builds intonation fast.
  • Listen for beats. Roughness = out of tune; smoothness = in tune. Train your ear to want the smoothness.
  • Return to Sa constantly — after every phrase, touch Sa to confirm you haven’t drifted.
  • Record yourself with the drone and play it back; you’ll hear drift you missed live.
  • Use headphones for fine work, and keep one consistent Sa so the pitch becomes instinctive.
Caring for a physical shruti box / harmonium: pump the bellows slowly and evenly for a steady tone, keep the reeds free of dust, don’t force the bellows, and store it flat in a dry place away from damp and direct heat so the reeds and bellows cloth last.
